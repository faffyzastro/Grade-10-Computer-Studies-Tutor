
# STRAND 1.0: FOUNDATION OF COMPUTER STUDIES

## 1.1 Evolution of Computers
**Time Allocation:** 8 Lessons

### Sub-strand Overview:
This sub-strand traces the journey from manual calculation tools to modern electronic computers.

### Essential Knowledge & Skills:
* **Pre-Mechanical Era:** Understanding tools like the Abacus, Napier's Bones, and the Pascaline.
* **Mechanical Era:** Focused on Charles Babbage’s Difference Engine and Analytical Engine.
* **Electronic Eras (Generations):**
    * **1st Generation:** Vacuum Tubes (e.g., ENIAC, UNIVAC). Characterized by large size and high heat.
    * **2nd Generation:** Transistors. Faster, smaller, and more reliable.
    * **3rd Generation:** Integrated Circuits (ICs). Introduction of keyboards and monitors.
    * **4th Generation:** Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) / Microprocessors. The era of Personal Computers (PCs).
    * **5th Generation:** Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Parallel Processing.

## 1.2 Computer Architecture
**Time Allocation:** 10 Lessons

### Sub-strand Overview:
Learners explore the internal logical structure of a computer system, primarily based on the **Von Neumann Architecture**.

### Key Concepts:
* **Functional Units:** * **Input Unit:** Accepts data.
    * **Central Processing Unit (CPU):** The brain of the system.
    * **Output Unit:** Presents processed information.
    * **Storage Unit:** Holds data and instructions.
* **The Fetch-Execute Cycle:** The process by which the CPU retrieves a program instruction from its memory, determines what the instruction is, and carries it out.

## 1.3 Input and Output (I/O) Devices
**Time Allocation:** 12 Lessons

### Sub-strand Overview:
Identification and selection of appropriate hardware based on specific user needs.

### Device Categories:
* **Advanced Input:** Beyond keyboards/mice, including Scanners, Digital Cameras, QR Code readers, and Biometric sensors.
* **Output Devices:**
    * **Soft Copy:** Monitors (LCD, LED, OLED), Projectors, and Speakers.
    * **Hard Copy:** Impact vs. Non-Impact printers (Laser, Inkjet, Thermal, 3D Printers).
* **Selection Criteria:** Speed, Cost, Resolution, and Portability.

## 1.4 Computer Storage
**Time Allocation:** 10 Lessons

### Key Learning Outcomes:
By the end of this sub-strand, the learner should be able to:
1.  Distinguish between **Primary** and **Secondary** storage.
2.  Describe the characteristics of RAM (Volatile) vs. ROM (Non-volatile).
3.  Analyze secondary storage technologies:
    * **Magnetic:** Hard Disk Drives (HDD).
    * **Optical:** CDs, DVDs, Blu-ray.
    * **Solid State:** SSDs, Flash disks, SD cards.
    * **Cloud Storage:** Remote data management.

## 1.5 Central Processing Unit (CPU)
**Time Allocation:** 8 Lessons

### Component Breakdown:
* **Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU):** Performs mathematical calculations and logical comparisons.
* **Control Unit (CU):** Directs the flow of data between the CPU and other devices.
* **Registers:** High-speed storage locations within the CPU (e.g., Program Counter, Accumulator).
* **System Clock:** Determines the speed at which the CPU executes instructions (measured in GHz).

STRAND 2.0: COMPUTER NETWORKING
2.1 Introduction to Data Communication
Time Allocation: 8 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Learners define the basic components and processes involved in transmitting data between two or more points.

Key Concepts:
Elements of Data Communication:

Message: The information to be communicated.

Sender: The device that sends the data.

Receiver: The device that receives the data.

Medium: The physical path (wired or wireless).

Protocol: The set of rules governing data exchange.

Data Transmission Modes:

Simplex: One-way communication (e.g., Radio/TV).

Half-Duplex: Two-way, but one at a time (e.g., Walkie-talkie).

Full-Duplex: Simultaneous two-way communication (e.g., Mobile phone).

Protocols & Standards: Introduction to the OSI Model (7 Layers) and TCP/IP suite.

2.2 Data Transmission Media
Time Allocation: 12 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Exploration of the physical and "invisible" pathways used for networking.

Media Categories:
Bounded (Guided) Media:

Twisted Pair: UTP and STP cables (commonly used in LANs).

Coaxial Cable: Used for cable TV and older networks.

Fiber Optic: Uses light pulses; offers the highest bandwidth and immunity to electromagnetic interference.

Unbounded (Unguided) Media:

Radio Waves: Used for Wi-Fi and Bluetooth.

Microwaves: Terrestrial and Satellite communication.

Infrared: Short-range communication (e.g., Remote controls).

Comparison Factors: Bandwidth, attenuation, cost, and ease of installation.

2.3 Computer Network Elements
Time Allocation: 10 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Identifying the hardware and software required to build a functional network.

Key Hardware:
Network Interface Card (NIC): The physical connection point for a computer.

Hub vs. Switch: Understanding how a Switch is "smarter" because it sends data only to the intended recipient.

Router: Connects different networks (e.g., your home LAN to the Internet).

Gateway: A node that joins two networks with different protocols.

Access Point (AP): Allows wireless devices to connect to a wired network.

Network Types:
LAN (Local Area Network): Small area like a lab or office.

CAN (Campus Area Network): Interconnected LANs within a specific geographical area (e.g., a school).

MAN (Metropolitan Area Network): Covers a city.

WAN (Wide Area Network): Covers countries or continents (e.g., The Internet).

2.4 Computer Network Topologies
Time Allocation: 10 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
The physical or logical arrangement of computers in a network.

Common Topologies:
Star Topology: All nodes connect to a central hub/switch. If the hub fails, the whole network fails; if one node fails, the rest stay up.

Bus Topology: All nodes share a single backbone cable. Cheap but difficult to troubleshoot.

Ring Topology: Each node connects to exactly two others. Data travels in one direction.

Mesh Topology: Every node is connected to every other node. Extremely reliable but expensive.

Hybrid Topology: A combination of two or more topologies.


2.5 Network Security and Ethics
Time Allocation: 6 Lessons

Essential Knowledge:
Threats: Unauthorized access, viruses/malware, and phishing.

Security Measures: Firewalls, encryption, strong passwords, and Biometrics.

Ethics: Netiquette, respecting privacy, and avoiding cyberbullying or plagiarism.

STRAND 3.0: SOFTWARE DEVELOPMENT
3.1 Introduction to Programming Concepts
Time Allocation: 8 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Learners define what programming is and the different levels of languages used to communicate with hardware.

Key Concepts:
Levels of Programming Languages:

Low-Level Languages (LLL): Machine language (binary) and Assembly language (using mnemonics). Closest to hardware, fast but difficult for humans.

High-Level Languages (HLL): Languages like Python, Java, and C++. They use English-like commands and are portable across different systems.

Language Translators:

Assembler: Converts assembly code to machine code.

Compiler: Translates the entire source code into an executable file at once (e.g., C++).

Interpreter: Translates and executes code line-by-line (e.g., Python).

3.2 Program Development Process
Time Allocation: 14 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
The systematic steps taken to create a functional software application.

The 5 Stages of Development:
Problem Analysis: Defining the requirements and constraints.

Program Design: Planning the logic using tools like:

Algorithms: A step-by-step procedure for solving a problem.

Pseudocode: A narrative, informal way of describing program logic.

Flowcharts: Graphical representations of logic using standard symbols (Start/End, Input/Output, Process, Decision).

Coding: Writing the actual instructions in a programming language (Python).

Testing and Debugging: Finding and fixing errors (Syntax, Logical, and Runtime errors).

Documentation and Maintenance: Writing manuals and updating the software.

3.3 Basic Programming Constructs (Python)
Time Allocation: 20 Lessons

Key Learning Outcomes:
By the end of this sub-strand, the learner should be able to write simple Python scripts.

Essential Syntax:
Identifiers and Variables: Rules for naming (e.g., must start with a letter, no spaces).

Data Types: * int (Integers)

float (Decimals)

str (Strings/Text)

bool (True/False)

Operators:

Arithmetic: +, -, *, /, % (modulus), ** (power).

Relational: ==, !=, >, <, >=, <=.

Logical: and, or, not.

3.4 Control Structures
Time Allocation: 18 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Directing the "flow" of the program based on conditions or repetitions.

1. Selection (Decision Making):
if statements: Executing code only if a condition is true.

if...else: Providing an alternative path.

elif: Checking multiple conditions.

2. Iteration (Looping):
for loops: Used when the number of iterations is known (e.g., "Repeat 10 times").

while loops: Used when the loop continues as long as a condition is true.

3.5 Data Structures
Time Allocation: 12 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Organizing and storing data efficiently in memory.

Python Collections:
Lists: Ordered, changeable, and allows duplicate members. Syntax: my_list = [1, 2, 3].

Tuples: Ordered and unchangeable. Syntax: my_tuple = (1, 2, 3).

Dictionaries: Unordered, changeable, and indexed by keys. Syntax: my_dict = {"name": "Kamau", "age": 16}.

3.6 Functions and Modularity
Time Allocation: 10 Lessons

Key Concepts:
Defining Functions: Using the def keyword to create reusable blocks of code.

Parameters and Arguments: Passing data into functions.

Return Values: Getting data back from a function.

Benefits: Reduces code repetition, makes debugging easier, and improves readability.

STRAND 4.0: ICT AND SOCIETY
4.1 Evolution of ICT
Time Allocation: 6 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Learners examine how Information and Communication Technology has moved from basic tools to an integrated global infrastructure.

Key Concepts:
The Digital Revolution: Transition from analog and mechanical electronic technology to digital electronics.

Impact on Services: How ICT has transformed Banking (M-Pesa/Online banking), Education (E-learning), and Governance (E-citizen).

The Digital Divide: The gap between demographics and regions that have access to modern ICT and those that don't.

4.2 Emerging Technologies
Time Allocation: 10 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
An exploration of cutting-edge technologies that are currently shaping the future of the industry.

Key Technologies:
Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning: Systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence.

Internet of Things (IoT): A network of physical objects ("things") embedded with sensors and software to exchange data over the internet.

Cloud Computing: On-demand availability of computer system resources, especially data storage and computing power.

Blockchain: A decentralized, distributed digital ledger technology.

Data Science and Big Data: Analyzing extremely large data sets to reveal patterns and trends.

4.3 Ethics and Cyber Security
Time Allocation: 8 Lessons

Sub-strand Overview:
Focuses on the moral principles and legal frameworks governing the use of computers.

Legal and Ethical Frameworks:
Data Protection Act (Kenya): Understanding the rights of data subjects and the obligations of data controllers.

Computer Misuse and Cybercrimes Act: Legal consequences for hacking, unauthorized access, and cyber-bullying.

Intellectual Property Rights (IPR): Understanding Copyright, Patents, and Trademarks in the digital space.

Software Piracy: The illegal copying, distribution, or use of software.

4.4 Green Computing and Health
Time Allocation: 4 Lessons

Key Learning Outcomes:
Environmental Impact: Understanding E-waste management and the importance of energy-efficient hardware.

Ergonomics: Designing a workspace for comfort and health (e.g., monitor height, chair support).

Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI): Preventing physical issues caused by prolonged computer use.
