## Strand 1
Sub-strand 1.1: Introduction to Biology

**Time Allocation:** 8 Lessons

**Key Inquiry Questions:**
1. Why is the study of Biology important?
2. How does Biology relate to other subjects?

**Specific Learning Outcomes:**
* Explain the meaning of Biology and its branches
* Describe the importance of Biology in daily life
* Explore the characteristics of living organisms
* Relate Biology to other subjects and career opportunities

---

**Core Content:**
* **Definition:** Biology as the study of living things and their interactions with the environment.
* **Main Branches:**
    * **Botany:** Study of plants.
    * **Zoology:** Study of animals.
    * **Microbiology:** Study of microscopic organisms.
* **Importance of Biology:** Applications in Agriculture, Medicine, Environment, and Resource Management.
* **Characteristics of Living Organisms (MRS GREN):** Movement, Reproduction, Sensitivity, Growth, Respiration, Excretion, and Nutrition.
* **Interdisciplinary Links:** Relationship with Chemistry, Physics, Geography, and Mathematics.

---

**Suggested Learning Experiences:**
* Brainstorming the meaning and importance of Biology in peer groups.
* Field observation: Identifying characteristics of organisms in the immediate environment.
* Exploring career paths and branches using digital resources and animations.

**Suggested Assessment Methods:**
* **Oral Questioning:** Regarding branches and the significance of Biology.
* **Written Tests:** Covering characteristics of life and interdisciplinary relationships.
* **Practical Observation:** Checking learners' ability to identify life characteristics in situ.

---

## Sub-strand 1.2: Classification

**Time Allocation:** 12 Lessons

**Key Inquiry Questions:**
1. Why is classification important in Biology?
2. How are organisms named scientifically?

**Specific Learning Outcomes:**
* Explain the meaning and importance of classification in Biology
* Describe the principles of binomial nomenclature
* Use a magnifying lens to observe organisms
* Appreciate the diversity of organisms in the environment

---

**Core Content:**
* **Meaning of Classification:** Grouping of organisms based on shared characteristics.
* **Importance of Classification:** To avoid confusion, identify organisms, and show evolutionary relationships.
* **Magnifying Lens:** Use and care; calculating magnification ($Magnification = \frac{\text{Size of Image}}{\text{Size of Object}}$).
* **Binomial Nomenclature:** Double naming system using Genus and Species (e.g., *Homo sapiens*); rules for writing scientific names.
* **Taxonomic Units:** Kingdom, Phylum/Division, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species.

---

**Suggested Learning Experiences:**
* Grouping various items/objects to understand the principle of classification.
* Using a magnifying lens to observe external features of plants and animals.
* Practicing naming common organisms using binomial nomenclature.
* Watching animations/digital resources on the history and development of classification.

**Suggested Assessment Methods:**
* **Oral Questioning:** On the importance of classification and rules of binomial nomenclature.
* **Written Tests:** Naming organisms and calculating magnification.
* **Observation:** Assessing the learner's skill in using and handling a magnifying lens.

---

## Sub-strand 1.3: The Cell

**Time Allocation:** 16 Lessons

**Key Inquiry Questions:**
1. Why is the cell considered the basic unit of life?
2. How does the structure of a microscope relate to its function?

**Specific Learning Outcomes:**
* Explain the meaning of a cell and the cell theory
* Identify parts of a light microscope and state their functions
* Use and care for a light microscope correctly
* Calculate magnification of a light microscope
* Prepare temporary slides of plant and animal cells
* Estimate cell size

---

**Core Content:**
* **The Cell:** Definition and introduction to cell theory.
* **The Light Microscope:**
    * **Parts:** Eyepiece, objective lenses, stage, mirror/light source, diaphragm, coarse and fine adjustment knobs. 
    * **Functions:** Magnification and resolution.
    * **Care and Handling:** Proper storage and cleaning.
* **Practical Skills:**
    * **Magnification Calculation:** Total magnification = Eyepiece lens magnification $\times$ Objective lens magnification.
    * **Slide Preparation:** Making temporary wet mounts of plant cells (e.g., onion epidermis) and animal cells (e.g., cheek cells).
    * **Cell Size Estimation:** Using the field of view to estimate actual dimensions.

---

**Suggested Learning Experiences:**
* Discussing the cell as the basic unit of life and the tenets of cell theory.
* Hands-on activity: Identifying parts of the light microscope and practicing focusing.
* Preparing and observing temporary slides under the microscope.
* Watching animations on microscope functions and cell size estimation.

**Suggested Assessment Methods:**
* **Practical Observation:** Checking the learner's ability to prepare slides and focus the microscope.
* **Drawing and Labeling:** Evaluating diagrams of observed cells and microscope parts.
* **Written Tests:** On magnification calculations and cell theory.

---

| :--- | :--- |
| **Exceeding Expectations** | Accurately identifies all microscope parts; prepares high-quality slides independently; calculates magnification and cell size precisely. |
| **Meeting Expectations** | Identifies microscope parts; prepares temporary slides; calculates magnification and estimates cell size correctly. |
| **Approaching Expectations** | Identifies some microscope parts; prepares slides and calculates magnification with support. |
| **Below Expectations** | Identifies a few microscope parts; requires significant support to use the microscope or prepare slides. |

---

**Non-Formal Activities & Resources:**
* **Activity:** Microscope maintenance workshops or "Micro-photography" sessions to document observed specimens.
* **Resources:** Light microscopes, blank slides, cover slips, stains (Iodine, Methylene blue), onion bulbs, toothpicks (for cheek cells), Digital resources.

## Sub-strand 1.4: Chemicals of Life

**Time Allocation:** 12 Lessons

**Key Inquiry Questions:**
1. Why are chemical compounds important in living organisms?
2. How can various food substances be identified?

**Specific Learning Outcomes:**
* Explain the meaning of "chemicals of life"
* Classify chemical compounds in living organisms into organic and inorganic
* Identify the chemical elements in carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins
* Carry out food tests for various chemical compounds
* Appreciate the importance of chemical compounds in living organisms

---

**Core Content:**
* **Meaning of Chemicals of Life:** Chemical substances that make up living organisms and enable them to carry out life processes.
* **Classification:**
    * **Inorganic Compounds:** Water and Mineral salts.
    * **Organic Compounds:** Carbohydrates, Lipids, Proteins, and Nucleic acids.
* **Nutrient Composition:**
    * **Carbohydrates:** Consist of Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen.
    * **Lipids:** Consist of Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen.
    * **Proteins:** Consist of Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, and sometimes Sulfur/Phosphorus.
* **Practical Skills (Food Tests):**
    * Starch (Iodine test).
    * Reducing and Non-reducing sugars (Benedict’s test).
    * Proteins (Biuret test).
    * Lipids (Emulsion/Grease spot test).

---

**Suggested Learning Experiences:**
* Discussing the meaning and classification of chemical compounds in living organisms.
* Conducting experiments to test for the presence of various food substances.
* Watching animations/digital resources on the structure and importance of chemicals of life.

**Suggested Assessment Methods:**
* **Practical Observation:** Evaluating the learner’s ability to perform food tests and record color changes.
* **Oral Questioning:** Regarding the composition and importance of organic compounds.
* **Written Tests:** On classification of compounds and chemical elements.

* Relate the structure of the heart and blood vessels to their functions.
* Explain the process of blood circulation in humans.
* Appreciate the importance of the circulatory system in maintaining health.

---

**Core Content:**
* **Components of the Circulatory System:** The heart, blood vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries), and blood.
* **The Human Heart:** * Structure: Atria, ventricles, valves, and major blood vessels (Aorta, Vena Cava, Pulmonary artery, and Pulmonary vein).
    * Adaptations: Thick muscular walls (especially the left ventricle) and presence of valves to prevent backflow.
    

[Image of the human heart structure]

* **Blood Vessels:** Structural differences between arteries, veins, and capillaries.
* **Blood:** Composition (Plasma, Red blood cells, White blood cells, Platelets) and their respective functions.
* **Circulation:** Pulmonary and systemic circulation.
* **Health Issues:** Common circulatory disorders (e.g., hypertension, varicose veins, heart attack) and preventive measures.

* Dissecting a mammalian heart (e.g., goat or cow) to identify internal structures.
* Observing prepared slides of blood smears under a microscope to identify blood cells.

* Using digital resources or animations to trace the path of blood through the body.

**Suggested Assessment Methods:**
* **Oral Questioning:** On the functions of heart parts and blood components.
* **Written Tests:** Covering the differences between blood vessels and the path of circulation.

## Sub-strand 3.2: Reproductive Health

**Time Allocation:** 12 Lessons

**Key Inquiry Questions:**
1. How can reproductive health be maintained?
2. Why is it important to seek medical advice on reproductive health?

---

**Specific Learning Outcomes:**
* Explain the meaning of reproductive health.
* Identify common reproductive tract infections (RTIs) and their causes.
* Describe the symptoms, prevention, and management of RTIs.
* Discuss the importance of reproductive health in human life.
* Appreciate the role of counseling and medical advice in reproductive health.

---

**Core Content:**
* **Meaning of Reproductive Health:** A state of physical, mental, and social well-being in all matters relating to the reproductive system.
* **Reproductive Tract Infections (RTIs):**
    * **Causes:** Bacteria, viruses, and fungi.
    * **Examples:** Syphilis, Gonorrhea, Candidiasis, and Trichomoniasis.
* **Symptoms and Management:**
    * Common symptoms: Unusual discharge, itching, sores, and pain during urination.
    * Prevention: Abstinence, being faithful to one partner, and proper hygiene.
    * Management: Seeking early medical diagnosis and completing prescribed treatment.
* **Sexual Health and Counseling:** Importance of guidance and professional counseling in making informed decisions.

---

**Suggested Learning Experiences:**
* Discussing the meaning and components of reproductive health in small groups.
* Using digital resources or case studies to identify symptoms and prevention of various RTIs.
* Role-playing scenarios on seeking medical advice and counseling for reproductive health issues.
* Inviting a health professional to talk about reproductive health and hygiene.

**Suggested Assessment Methods:**
* **Oral Questioning:** On the causes and symptoms of common RTIs.
* **Written Tests:** Covering prevention measures and the definition of reproductive health.
* **Role Play/Observation:** Assessing the learner's ability to communicate the importance of seeking medical help.
